🤝 Level 3 · Starting Conversations

Community activities to kickstart conversations with my audience

To bring energy transition conversations down to earth, we need community spaces that invite citizens to connect through lived experience.

💡 To make the energy transition real and relatable, we need to create spaces where citizens can talk, reflect, and imagine together what this transition means for their daily lives.

Below are formats that The Good Chat and partners have tested successfully across Southeast Asia, along with global examples. Each is easy to adapt, replicate, and combine depending on your community's size, context, and resources.
PS: don't hesitate to contribute to this list by sharing your event formats with us 😊
✏️
Journaling Prompts
Reflection before conversation
›

Objective: Help participants connect personally with energy and climate before engaging in group dialogue.

How it works

Share 2–3 short writing prompts and give participants 10–15 minutes to reflect and write freely. Then invite them to share one insight, surprise, or feeling that emerged.

"When was the last time I thought about how much energy I use?"
"What part of my daily routine depends most on electricity?"
"How much do I worry about my energy costs?"
Why it works

Journaling lowers the pressure to have the "right answer." It makes the energy transition personal, grounded in lived experience, and sets the tone for open, thoughtful discussion.

Inspired by climate circles and "inner transition" practices used by Wellbeing Journey (Indonesia), the Transition Towns Movement (UK), and Climate Cafés (EU).

📚
Book Clubs
Leverage books to make people care about climate and energy
›

Objective: Use reading as a starting point for reflection and dialogue — helping communities connect familiar topics like parenting, family values, and everyday care with broader issues like climate change and the energy transition.

How it works
  • Invite participants to choose and read books related to topics your community cares about.
  • During the book club, guide members to reflect through a new lens: "How do the values in this book connect to caring for our planet?"
  • Encourage participants to share personal experiences, linking lessons from the book to real-life challenges like air pollution, rising temperatures, or energy use at home.
  • Organize book challenges or competitions where members nominate, read, and discuss multiple books — each time drawing climate and energy connections.
🇮🇩
Puty Puar, founder of Indonesia's 90,000-member Buibu Baca Buku community, invited mothers to read books on motherhood, religion, and education through the climate lens. She launched book competitions that rewarded active readers and storytellers — turning her community's passion for books into a powerful engine of climate literacy.
Why it works

Book clubs create safe, trusted spaces where participants explore new ideas through stories that already feel relevant to their lives.

🌐
Online Surveys, Story Boxes & Q&A Sessions
Listening at scale
›

Objective: Collect public perceptions, stories, and questions about energy in accessible, low-barrier ways.

How it works
  • Launch a short survey on Instagram Stories asking: "What's your biggest concern about electricity costs?" or "Would you install solar panels if it were affordable?"
  • Create a story box or open Q&A space where followers can share experiences anonymously.
  • Host a live Q&A session with an expert or local advocate to respond to common questions.
🇹🇭
Maria Lynn Ehren (1M+ followers) used Instagram Stories surveys and anonymous questionnaires to understand her audience's attitudes on energy and climate. The results were striking:
92%
concerned about rising electricity costs
80%
trying to cut energy use
63.8%
know fossil fuels cause climate change

This tactic shows that conversations at scale are possible. When designed intentionally, surveys surface collective narratives and invite people into the climate conversation on their own terms.

Why it works

These activities make people feel heard while giving organizers valuable insights. They also create data and testimonials that can feed future campaigns.

The Good Chat Card Game
Turning small talks into courageous talks
›

Objective: Help people start meaningful, relatable conversations about the energy transition — anywhere, with anyone.

What it is

A conversation-based learning tool designed to make discussions about climate and energy feel natural, personal, and engaging. The game includes question cards and scenario prompts that guide players to reflect on habits, values, and hopes — while gradually linking them to larger systemic issues like clean energy and sustainability.

Learning goals
  • Demystify the energy transition by breaking it into personal, accessible conversations
  • Build empathy and curiosity — helping people listen to each other's perspectives
  • Connect the personal and the systemic — showing how daily experiences relate to global change
  • Normalize climate conversations so talking about energy becomes part of everyday life
How it works

Played in small groups of 4–8 people, in person or online. Sessions last 30–90 minutes. Facilitators select cards depending on the context — community event, classroom, or digital session.

Why it works

The format is inclusive, low-pressure, and fun. It doesn't aim to convince people — it aims to make them care and talk. These repeated, honest conversations between friends, colleagues, or strangers help create the social momentum needed for collective action.

👥
Small Group Deep Dives
Focused gatherings around everyday energy concerns
›

Objective: Engage small groups around practical, relatable topics — guiding them to see how personal struggles connect to the larger energy system.

What it is

Intimate, discussion-based events (10–25 participants) that begin with a concrete problem — aircon bills, fuel prices, electric vehicles — and evolve into conversations about how systemic change could make life easier for everyone.

How it works
1
Start where people are. Open with the practical question that brought them together: "How can we save on electricity bills?" or "Is it worth investing in an EV?"
2
Let participants share experiences. These stories build trust and reveal how many people face the same constraints — high bills, limited options, unfair pricing.
3
Invite a speaker who can connect the dots. A relatable expert — designer, engineer, influencer, or homeowner — helps people see these issues are symptoms of a system that still relies on expensive, imported, and polluting energy.
4
End with a sense of direction. Ask: "What could change if we pushed for fairer energy pricing or local solar access?" Share examples of collective solutions and encourage participants to host the next conversation.
✨
This format worked beautifully in the Living Loving × Good Chat collaboration, where a discussion about cooling costs evolved into a conversation about energy justice and sustainable housing.

Event Ideas & Formats

Seven ready-to-run event formats — adapt them to your community, context, and style.

💨 Cool Smarter: The True Cost of Aircon
A discussion on how air conditioning drives household expenses and emissions. Participants share cooling hacks while a guest speaker explains how energy efficiency and national power systems shape what we pay. Ends with a reflection on how better policy and grid access could make comfort affordable for all.
🚗 Drive the Future: Electric Vehicles — good for me and the planet?
Start with curiosity around EVs — cost, charging, performance — then guide the conversation toward what's needed to make EVs accessible beyond big cities: renewable grids, charging infrastructure, and fair transition policies.
🏠 Home Energy Consumption Diary
A journaling-based meet-up where participants track their energy use for a week, then discuss what surprised them most. Facilitators link personal habits to national energy dependence and open a discussion on how citizens can advocate for local solar and energy reforms.
🔌 Plugged In: Why Does Our Electricity Cost So Much?
An explainer-style session with an energy analyst breaking down where your electricity bill really goes — generation, import, infrastructure, and fossil fuel subsidies — followed by a community discussion on how citizens can push for fairer, cleaner energy pricing.
☀️ Is Rooftop Solar Really Revolutionary in My Neighborhood?
Invite 2–3 local residents or entrepreneurs who have installed solar panels to share their experiences. Use their stories to open a conversation on how citizens can become energy producers and what policy changes would make it easier.
🛵 How to Reduce My Commuting Costs?
A casual session for motorcycle riders, delivery workers, and commuters exploring rising fuel costs, e-bikes, and public transport options. A guest speaker connects these struggles to the larger need for sustainable mobility policies and clean energy infrastructure.
💡 Energy Myths & Realities
A myth-busting Q&A session with an expert or influencer answering common misconceptions about renewables, costs, and access. Encourages participants to share their own questions — turning skepticism into collective learning.
