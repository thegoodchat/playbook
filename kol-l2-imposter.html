💪 Level 2 · Your Entry Point

How to fight imposter syndrome

You don't need to be an energy expert to advocate. You just need to care about fairness, access, and a livable future.

What is imposter syndrome? Why your voice matters Exercises A bigger question

What is imposter syndrome?

Imposter syndrome is the voice in your head that says you don't deserve to speak. It makes you feel like you are not good enough — even when you are.

As a KOL, it often sounds like this:

😔"I'm not a scientist, so maybe I shouldn't talk about climate change."
😔"Other people know more than me."
😔"What if I say something wrong?"

This feeling is common. Many leaders and creators experience it. And here's the thing: having imposter syndrome actually means you care about doing a good job. It's proof that you are pushing yourself to grow.

✅ Green flag
Harvard professor Arthur C. Brooks says imposter syndrome isn't a flaw — it's literally a green flag. His advice? Don't shove it away. Don't spiral. Just lean in to the feeling. Use it as fuel to keep getting better.

So if you ever think, "who let me in this room?"… congrats. It means you're exactly where you're supposed to be.

Reframe the Fear

When that fear shows up, don't let it take over. Give it a new script.

Instead of… Try this →
"I'm not qualified." "I'm here to connect, not to lecture."
"Others know more than me." "I bring a perspective they don't have."
"What if I make a mistake?" "I can always learn and improve."

Think of that nervous energy before you hit "post." Your heart beats fast, your finger hovers over the button. Reframe it: that nervousness means you care. Remind yourself — "I'm not here to be perfect. I'm here to connect."

Why your voice matters

Your voice isn't "just another opinion." It's something scientists often can't do. Here's why:

01
Relatable + Trusted
Scientists are smart, but far away. You're right here. People know you — your posts, your wins, your worries. When you share a message, it doesn't feel like a lecture. It feels like a friend talking.
02
You Tell Stories
Science talks in charts and numbers. You make it a story — a rainy day that felt off-season, a convo with your mom, a flooded street. People remember feelings, not numbers.
03
You Make It Simple
You don't need to explain the carbon cycle. You just translate: "The air is like a blanket. It's getting thicker. That's why it's hotter and storms are more intense." See?
04
You Reach People Where They Are
Scientists write in journals. You hang out on TikTok, IG, YouTube — where people scroll every day. You can drop a climate story in a feed where no one expected it, and boom, it clicks.
05
You Inspire Action
Knowing stuff is nice. Doing stuff is better. You can show tiny steps — try this swap, go to this event, meet this local expert. You don't just share the problem. You share what we can do.
💡
Lived experience = legit data
You don't have to hide the doubt. Start with: "I'm not a scientist, but here's what I've noticed…" That flip turns imposter voice into honesty + relatability.

Exercises to Find Your Climate Voice

Try one. Try all four. They're quick, practical, and designed to turn your lived experience into your superpower.

🚶 Observation Walk ›

Take a short walk around your neighborhood. Notice 3 things connected to weather or climate.

Is it hotter than usual? Is the air hazy? Are plants or seasons acting differently?

Jot them down or snap a quick pic. That's your raw material — the starting point for a post only you can write.
🎛️ Fact + POV Remix ›

Take one science fact you've read and add your personal perspective.

Fact: "Sea levels are rising."
You: "My family's riverside house now floods almost every year."

Mixing facts with personal experience makes your voice powerful and credible.
🎤 Pass the Mic ›

Use your platform to highlight another voice. Share a quote, clip, or post from a community directly impacted and add:

"Their perspective matters — here's why I'm sharing it."

This shows you're learning out loud, not speaking over. It positions you as a connector, not a lecturer.
✍️ Lived Experience = Legit Data ›

Write one short post starting with one of these openers:

"I'm not a scientist, but here's what I've noticed…"

or

"As someone living here, I've noticed…"

This flips the imposter voice into honesty + relatability. Your doubt becomes your authenticity.
Just a thought
Why do we still feel like guests in our own narratives?
👉Who benefits if we stay silent?
👉Who might feel less alone if we speak?

Imposter syndrome, in its original form, described high-achieving women who felt undeserving of success because of external bias and exclusion. Over time, it got reframed as a personal flaw — when really, it's a symptom of systemic injustice.

When you post online, especially in spaces shaped by Western norms, that voice of doubt isn't proof you're unqualified. It's the echo of structures built to make you feel that way. Your presence, your local lens, your humor, cultural wisdom, and storytelling — it's already an act of resistance.

Our work is not to "fix" ourselves. It's to unlearn the doubt, build collective confidence, and create spaces where diverse voices thrive.
