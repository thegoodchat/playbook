🪞 Level 2 · Your Entry Point

Your Story, Your Why — Finding Your Place in the Movement

It starts with you. When you turn your story into a message, your voice becomes a force for collective change.

1
Identifying your 'why' — connecting personal experience and values to the climate movement
2
Finding your unique voice — the first steps in identifying a natural, authentic way to discuss climate topics
3
A mini-list of civic actions — to integrate into regular online and offline dialogue
We often think of the climate movement as something big and far away — something led by scientists, activists, or policymakers. But honestly? It's much closer than we think.

Sometimes it begins with something small. The way the air smells before a storm. The street in Bangkok flooding again after one heavy rain. The rising cost of food that used to feel so normal.

Or maybe it's not dramatic at all. Just that quiet ache when you realize the world you love might not stay the same.

That's where purpose starts — from noticing what matters to you.
Step 1 Begin with your personal story ›

Forget the statistics for a moment. What was your turning point?

Was it seeing your neighborhood in Jakarta flooded one too many times? Watching fishermen in your hometown struggle to predict the tides? Or maybe it was something gentler — like remembering how your grandma saved every scrap of food because she understood the value of enough.

These moments aren't random. They're clues to your why. When you speak from them — with honesty, vulnerability, even humor — you help others see themselves in the story. That's where connection begins.

Step 2 Identify your values — your inner compass ›

Ask yourself: What makes me angry, or protective, or hopeful? What kind of world do I want to help build?

Care
For people, food, craft, or community. Seeing how some in Manila live with blackouts or flooding while others barely notice.
Fairness
Standing up for those most affected by floods, heatwaves, and rising costs of living.
Creativity
Finding beauty in repair, reinvention, reuse. Reimagining how we live, design, and consume.

When you name your values, you start to see how they naturally link to the climate movement. You don't have to force it. Your values are already your connection point.

Step 3 Connect the personal to the bigger picture ›

Once you know why you care, zoom out: What systems around you could change so that caring isn't just personal — it's possible for everyone in our countries?

If you're a chef
Supporting local farmers and pushing for fair food supply chains.
If you're a designer
Asking how brands can build circular production instead of endless consumption.
If you're a wellness creator
Exploring how access to clean air, safe water, and rest is a basic right — not a luxury.

Your story is the spark. But your influence can fuel systemic imagination — the kind that asks, "What if we all lived differently?"

Step 4 Lead with hope, but stay grounded ›

Hope isn't naïve. It's strategy.

When you show your community what trying looks like — biking through traffic, cutting down waste, joining a neighborhood clean-up — you're making climate action visible.

In Southeast Asia, we already know what resilience looks like. It's the street vendors adapting to the heat, the barangay that rebuilds after every storm, the kampung spirit that refuses to disappear.

That's the heart of systemic change — communities that never stop believing we can do better together.

Step 5 From me to we ›

Personal change alone won't fix the climate crisis — but it can shift culture. And culture is what opens the door to policy, innovation, and collective power.

When you share your story, others start to share theirs. When you collaborate — with farmers, teachers, city planners, fellow creators — the conversation expands.

What began as your why becomes our way forward. Change scales not from the top down, but from the inside out.

You don't have to be an activist to make an impact. You just have to care deeply enough to connect your story to the bigger one — the one we're all still writing.

Because movements aren't built on perfection. They're built on people — in Bangkok, Jakarta, Manila — who start where they are, use what they have, and lead with heart.

🔑 Marshall Ganz · Harvard
The Story of Self, Us and Now

To build an energy transition story that relates to your community, you must first find your personal story. The model we'll follow was developed by Marshall Ganz — a Harvard professor who has worked with people to help them become stronger organisers for change. He calls it "Public Narrative."

"A good public story is drawn from the series of choice points that have structured the 'plot' of your life — the challenges you faced, choices you made, and outcomes you experienced."

Mini-Lists of Accessible Civic Actions

Three tiers of action — pick what feels right for where you are right now.

Simple, story-worthy habits that normalize caring — easy to mention in casual talk, captions, or content.

ActionHow to include it in daily talk
Buy local / seasonal"Been loving how mangoes taste extra sweet this season — eating with the rhythm of nature just hits different."
Repair / reuse / swap"My tote's older than my Instagram. Still works fine."
Walk / bike / use public transport"I started taking the Skytrain again — weirdly peaceful when you don't rush."
Support small producers"Got this from a local farmer's market — feels good knowing who grew it."
Slow down consumption"Trying to wait 24 hours before I buy something online. Surprisingly freeing."
💬These open the door for climate conversation without heavy language — they model mindset, not moralizing.

Actions that shift from me to we — useful for creators who host, collaborate, or organize.

ActionHow to bring it up
Join / host a cleanup or tree planting"We're doing a small riverside cleanup this weekend — join if you're free!"
Neighborhood swap / food share"We traded snacks across the street yesterday — everyone brought something homemade."
Community fridge or pantry"Our local café set up a free-fridge corner. I drop off extras sometimes."
Skill-sharing / repair club"Started a fix-it Sunday — people bring things, and we learn together."
Local storytelling / mapping"We're collecting stories of old trees in our area. It's like memory-keeping for the neighborhood."
💬These sound like culture, not activism — ideal for normalizing civic engagement through warmth and belonging.

When your platform has reach — these spark reflection about systems, not just individuals.

ActionHow to make it accessible
Ask "Where did this come from?""Every time I buy something new, I try to picture the hands that made it."
Spotlight others on your platform"Met a young creator working on solar lamps for rural homes — stories like this deserve space."
Advocate gently"Wouldn't it be cool if Bangkok had more shade trees for cyclists?"
Collaborate with purpose"Next collab? Maybe with a brand that's rethinking packaging."
Join local discussions or petitions"Our district's testing flood sensors — small, but these things add up."
💬This set helps creators link lifestyle content with systemic imagination — framing civic action as part of modern creativity.
