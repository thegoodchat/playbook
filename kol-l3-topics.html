💡 Level 3 · Starting Conversations

Top Topics to start conversation & spark interest in the Energy Transition

Five proven conversation starters — topics tested over two years with 20+ KOLs in The Good Chat — that spark real curiosity and engagement around renewable energy.

👉 Each of these five topics has been tested and refined over two years with more than 20 Key Opinion Leaders in The Good Chat. They're not theoretical — they're what actually resonates with audiences and motivates people to engage.

Topic 1 of 5
Cost of Living
›

Across Southeast Asia, the conversation about energy often begins with the rising cost of living. Electricity bills, groceries, transport, and cooling systems are among households' biggest monthly expenses — and all are directly influenced by energy prices.

When fuel and electricity become more expensive, the cost of producing and transporting goods increases. This drives up food prices, transport fares, and service costs. When energy prices rise, nearly everything else follows.

In energy import-dependent countries like Thailand and the Philippines, fuel, gas, and power prices are among the highest in the region.

Think about it: people already care about energy — but always through the lens of cost. In Thailand, when electricity prices surge, the government typically caps rates before elections to ease dissatisfaction. Once elections pass, prices rise again. This pattern shows that public pressure works. The challenge is sustaining that pressure beyond short-term moments and turning it into long-term advocacy for fair, affordable, renewable energy.

By reducing dependence on imported fossil fuels and investing in local renewable energy, countries can make energy prices more predictable, food and transport more affordable, and households less vulnerable to global market shocks. The energy transition is not just an environmental priority — it's essential for economic security and social wellbeing.

❣️
Topic 2 of 5
Family & Future Planning
›

Across Southeast Asia, family remains the strongest source of motivation for change. When people think about the future, they think about their children, parents, or grandparents. Connecting the energy transition to family life makes the topic concrete, emotional, and deeply personal.

Two family groups consistently showed high engagement in Good Chat campaigns:

  • Young Parents — "Ensuring their children's future"
    Young parents respond strongly when energy and climate issues are framed through education, health, and aspiration. Indonesian author and illustrator Puty Puar explored how parenting decisions connect to larger issues like air pollution and energy — and ended up co-writing a children's book series on clean energy.
  • Adults caring for older relatives — "Linking health, longevity, and air quality"
    Fossil fuel-based energy contributes significantly to air pollution, a major risk factor for respiratory and cardiovascular diseases. In Thailand, Indonesia, and the Philippines, poor air quality is already reducing life expectancy. Clean energy is therefore a public health necessity that can improve quality of life for millions.

By framing renewable energy as a way to protect both the youngest and oldest members of society, communicators help families see the transition as a shared, cross-generational responsibility.

Topic 3 of 5
Local Solar Pride
›

Solar energy is often seen as a technological solution, but in Southeast Asia it represents something much deeper — local pride, self-reliance, and innovation.

In many ways, solar energy reflects long-standing Southeast Asian values of collective action and resourcefulness. Communities have always found ways to innovate locally — through cooperatives, DIY solutions, or neighborhood networks. When solar energy is framed as a continuation of this tradition, people see it not as a foreign technology but as a community-driven practice.

🇵🇭
Frances Bautista of Home Buddies sparked a national conversation by asking her 3M-member community how they were coping with high electricity costs. Thousands responded by sharing photos of their solar panels and installation experiences. Frances turned these stories into a Facebook documentary — showing that renewable energy is already within reach.

This demonstrates that solar energy can be a catalyst for community identity and participation — empowering citizens to move from passive consumers to active entrepreneurs in the energy system.

🌴
Topic 4 of 5
Sustainable Home
›

Across Southeast Asia's cities, the idea of "comfort" is evolving. For a growing number of urban homeowners, a truly comfortable home is no longer just one that's cool or modern — it's one that's sustainable, energy-efficient, and resilient.

As energy costs rise and temperatures increase, homeowners are becoming more conscious of how their choices impact quality of life and expenses. Air conditioning is now one of the largest household costs — and one of the biggest contributors to carbon emissions. As the climate gets warmer, this creates a vicious cycle: more cooling, higher bills, worsening heat.

Communities are responding with a solution-oriented approach — focusing on concrete improvements in home energy use: better ventilation, insulation, smart cooling systems, and rooftop solar. This resonates especially with homeowners active in neighborhood associations, where comfort, safety, and maintenance are already common topics.

In Thailand, new regulations prevent individuals from selling excess solar power back to the grid — limiting both affordability and motivation for households to invest in renewable solutions. For the energy transition to move forward, the definition of "modern living" needs to keep evolving: from high-consumption comfort to smart, efficient, and sustainable comfort.

🌱
Topic 5 of 5
Green Careers
›

In today's rapidly changing job market, one topic dominates conversations across Southeast Asia: job security.

With the rise of AI, automation, and remote work, many professionals are anxious about the future of employment. Traditional industries are shrinking, and even skilled roles are being replaced or redefined. Amid this uncertainty, the green economy stands out as one of the few sectors capable of offering both financial safety and social stability.

Unlike digital or service industries, green sectors — renewable energy, waste management, sustainable agriculture — are deeply rooted in local contexts. They generate jobs that cannot be automated or outsourced, while strengthening community resilience and national self-reliance.

30M+
new green jobs could be created in Southeast Asia by 2030 — from solar installation to climate-tech entrepreneurship. Source: World Economic Forum & The Bridgespan Group

For citizens, supporting the energy transition isn't only about environmental concern — it's an economic and social opportunity. It also means unlocking new pathways for those who want to move beyond being consumers and become creators — launching solar and clean energy SMEs in provinces and secondary cities.
