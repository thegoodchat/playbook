☀️ Level 2 · Your Entry Point

Why are people interested and excited about solar energy?

When we talk about solar energy, we're not only talking about electricity. We're talking about a powerful community tool that can drive local innovation.

🔑

Direct discussions about renewable energy — particularly solar energy — have consistently resonated with our fellows' audiences. Solar energy is widely seen as a source of national pride. Stories that celebrate local solar advancements are especially engaging because they feel close, real, and achievable.

🇵🇭
Case Study · Philippines
From high electricity bills to national pride

In Edition 1, several Good Chat fellows in the Philippines explored their communities' interest in solar energy by facilitating discussions on how solar power could be used to power homes.

Karl Bautista Frances Cabatuando Buji Babiera

These conversations engaged hundreds of people who saw solar as a key solution addressing real concerns: high electricity costs, an unstable grid, and a genuine interest from home improvement enthusiasts.

In Edition 2, Buji Babiera continued the conversation by sharing insights from his visits to solar farms across the Philippines. A new observation emerged — his community didn't just respond positively to solar energy. They began sharing about solar farms in their own provinces.

Many began viewing solar as a homegrown innovation — something that embodies Filipino ingenuity and nationalism. Not just a climate solution, but a source of national pride.

What started as a conversation about electricity costs became something much bigger: a community movement proud of what the Philippines is building.

🇹🇭
Case Study · Thailand
Joe from Jeban Family — telling local stories that move people

Joe took a unique approach to inspire his audience in Thailand to care about renewable energy. Instead of presenting abstract statistics or Western-based projects, he sought out real, local stories — of community leaders who had taken bold steps to make their communities energy-sufficient.

🏝️ Koh Jik — the island that built its own solar solution ›

Koh Jik is a small island that had faced decades of struggle to join Thailand's National Electric Grid. When their lobbying efforts failed, the islanders decided to take matters into their own hands.

They raised funds and embraced solar power — creating their own energy solution from scratch.

Joe's storytelling didn't focus on the technical aspects. He painted a vivid picture of the islanders' determination, their harmony with nature, and their belief in their community's ability to chart its own future.

His audience was captivated by this example of grassroots innovation — proof that sustainability could grow from the ground up. For many, it was a source of pride, showcasing Thai ingenuity and reinforcing the idea that solutions could come from within their own country.

🏠 Phi Suea House — off-grid living in Chiang Mai ›

The Phi Suea House in Chiang Mai is an off-grid home powered by solar hydrogen. More than just a sustainable marvel, it represents a modular concept born in Chiang Mai for residential developments — proof that construction projects could be sustainable and cheaper.

Joe's audience was intrigued not only by the technology but by the hopeful message: Thailand was already capable of producing environmentally friendly, cost-effective energy solutions.

✊ From stories to action — the Energy Justice Thailand petition ›

Joe used these stories as a bridge to introduce his audience to the Energy-Justice-Thailand petition, which advocates for greater access to renewable energy solutions in the country.

His storytelling transformed what might have been a dry call to action into something personal, relatable, and urgent. The stories gave people a reason to care — then gave them something to do about it.

💡 The takeaway for you as a KOL

Joe didn't position himself as an expert or lecturer. He became a bridge — connecting real stories to real people and turning conversations into action.

His ability to make sustainability feel local, achievable, and deeply personal has had a lasting impact. You don't need to understand every policy detail. You need to find the story that makes your audience feel something.

Solar energy is your entry point. What's the local story in your community?
